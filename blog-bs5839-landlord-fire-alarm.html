BS 5839 for Landlords: Fire Alarm Requirements Explained

Grade D, Grade A, LD1, LD2 — the terminology can be baffling. Here’s what each category actually means for your rental property, and what you’re legally required to have in place.

By DS Electrical · April 2026 · 9 min read

If you own a rental property in Somerset — whether it’s a single-let house in Wells, a small HMO in Frome, or a licensed house in multiple occupation in Bath or Shepton Mallet — you have a legal duty to provide adequate fire detection. The standard that governs how that detection is designed and installed is BS 5839. Get it right, and you have documented, certificated compliance. Get it wrong, and the consequences range from a prohibition notice to a £30,000 fine per breach — or worse, a personal injury claim following an incident.

This guide explains the BS 5839 grades and categories in plain English, maps them to the types of tenancy you’re likely running, and sets out what landlords across Mid Somerset need to do to stay on the right side of the rules.

Quick summary: BS 5839 Part 6 governs domestic properties; Part 1 governs commercial and larger HMOs. Most single-let rentals need Grade D2 LD2 (mains-interlinked alarms). Licensed HMOs with five or more occupants require a Grade A system with a central control panel, designed and installed to Part 1.

The two relevant parts of BS 5839

BS 5839 is a multi-part British Standard. For landlords, two parts matter:

  • BS 5839 Part 6 — fire detection and alarm systems in domestic premises. Covers single-family homes, bedsits, and small HMOs. This is the standard referenced by the Smoke and Carbon Monoxide Alarm (Amendment) Regulations 2022 and by most local authority licensing conditions.
  • BS 5839 Part 1 — fire detection and alarm systems in non-domestic premises. Required for licensed HMOs with five or more occupants, commercial buildings, and mixed-use premises. Systems under Part 1 require a formal design, a central control panel, and commissioning documentation.

Grades explained: D, B, and A

The Grade describes how the detection equipment is powered and interconnected — not how sophisticated the sensors are.

Grade D — mains-powered with battery backup

Individual mains-powered detectors or sounders with an integral battery backup. The most common requirement for single-let rentals and small HMOs. Grade D1 uses a primary (non-rechargeable) backup battery. Grade D2 uses a rechargeable battery — generally preferred for rental properties as it removes the landlord’s obligation to replace primary cells.

Grade B — panel-based with battery backup (no central monitoring)

A conventional control panel with loop wiring to detectors and sounders. Rarely used for domestic, but occasionally specified for smaller HMOs where the local authority wants central indication but not remote monitoring.

Grade A — fully panel-based system

A control panel connected to a dedicated circuit, with addressable or conventional detector loops, manual call points, sounders, and the option for remote monitoring or connection to a central receiving centre (CRC). This is the standard for licensed HMOs and commercial premises. Grade A systems must be designed to BS 5839 Part 1 and require a commissioning certificate.

Categories explained: LD1, LD2, LD3

The Category describes where detectors are placed — the coverage level. LD stands for “life protection, domestic.”

CategoryCoverageTypical application
LD1All areas where fire could start, including roof voids and floor voidsFull-coverage HMOs, commercial premises
LD2Escape routes + high-risk rooms (kitchen, living room, any room with sleeping risk)Most rental properties and HMOs
LD3Escape routes onlyMinimum acceptable — rarely sufficient for rental

Which grade and category does your rental need?

The requirement depends on the type of tenancy and local authority licensing conditions. The table below reflects current national guidance; always check your specific licence conditions, as councils can and do specify higher standards.

Property typeMinimum standardNotes
Single-let house or flatGrade D2 LD2Smoke alarms on each floor, heat alarm in kitchen, all interlinked
Small HMO (2–4 occupants, unlicensed)Grade D2 LD2 or LD1LD1 if more complex layout; check local licensing conditions
Licensed HMO (5+ occupants)Grade A, LD1 or LD2Control panel required; annual service and certificate mandatory
Commercial premises or mixed-useGrade A, Part 1 designFormal design document, commissioning certificate, CRC optional

For the majority of landlords in Radstock, Midsomer Norton, Castle Cary, Bruton, Street, and Wedmore with standard single-let stock, Grade D2 LD2 is the baseline — but if your property has been converted or has unusual layout, or if you’re taking on an HMO, it’s worth getting a formal fire risk assessment to confirm the correct specification before work starts.

The annual testing and certificate requirement

Installing the system is only the first step. BS 5839 Part 6 and Part 1 both require periodic inspection and testing to maintain compliance:

  • Grade D systems: Monthly test by the occupier (pressing the test button), plus an annual inspection by a competent person who issues a test certificate. The certificate is what your local authority or insurer will ask for at licence renewal.
  • Grade A systems: Quarterly visits to test manual call points, sounders, and detection devices (in rotation), plus a full annual inspection and commissioning-standard certificate. For licensed HMOs, the local authority will require this certificate with every licence renewal.

If you can’t produce a current test certificate for a licensed HMO, the council has grounds to issue a hazard notice under the Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS) — which can ultimately lead to a prohibition notice preventing you from letting the property at all.

What happens if you get it wrong

The risks of non-compliant fire detection are significant, and they compound:

  • Insurance invalidated: Most landlord insurance policies include a fire detection condition. If an alarm system doesn’t meet BS 5839 grade and category requirements, or has no current certificate, the insurer may decline a claim after a fire.
  • Council prohibition notices: Under the HHSRS, inadequate fire detection is a Category 1 hazard. Councils can issue improvement notices, emergency prohibition orders, and ultimately take over management of the property.
  • Fines up to £30,000 per breach: The Housing Act 2004 allows fines of up to £30,000 for each licensing offence — and having a non-compliant or absent fire alarm system is a qualifying offence in most licensing schemes.
  • Personal injury liability: If a tenant is injured or killed in a fire where detection was absent or inadequate, the landlord faces civil claims and potentially corporate manslaughter proceedings if the failure was systematic.

The practical point: A properly specified and certificated Grade D2 LD2 system for a single-let house costs a few hundred pounds installed — a fraction of one month’s rent. For a licensed HMO, a Grade A system and annual service contract is a predictable, budgetable cost that removes the compliance risk entirely. The maths are not close.
