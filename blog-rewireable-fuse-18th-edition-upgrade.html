Consumer Unit Upgrade: From Rewireable Fuses to 18th Edition in a Day

Millions of UK homes still run on rewireable fuse boards that haven't been touched in 30 years. Here's exactly what upgrading to a compliant 18th Edition consumer unit involves — and why most jobs are done and dusted before teatime.

By DS Electrical · 23 April 2026 · 7 min read

If your fuse board has a row of ceramic cartridges, or you recognise the term "rewireable fuse" because you've replaced the wire yourself, you have an older board that falls well short of current UK wiring regulations. That's not a scare tactic — it's just a fact. And the good news is that the solution is straightforward: a modern 18th Edition all-RCBO consumer unit, usually fitted in a single working day.

This guide walks you through everything: why rewireable boards are no longer acceptable, what the 18th Edition (BS 7671:2018) actually requires, what you get with a new board, what the job costs, and what happens on the day itself.

Why Rewireable Fuse Boards Are No Longer Acceptable

Rewireable fuse boards work on a simple principle: a thin piece of fuse wire bridges two terminals inside a ceramic holder. When a circuit draws more current than the wire can handle, it melts and breaks the circuit. Job done — in theory.

The problem is everything they cannot do:

  • No RCD protection. A Residual Current Device detects earth fault currents as small as 30mA and cuts power in under 40 milliseconds — fast enough to prevent a fatal electric shock. Rewireable boards have none. If you drill into a live cable or touch a faulty appliance, there is nothing standing between you and the current.
  • No discrimination between circuits. On a rewireable board, a fault anywhere on a shared circuit brings down every outlet on that circuit. A modern RCBO board isolates faults to a single circuit, leaving the rest of your home unaffected.
  • Wooden backboards. Many rewireable installations from the 1960s and 1970s are mounted on a wooden board inside a cupboard. Wood and electrical faults are a dangerous combination that has caused house fires.
  • Part P non-compliance. Any electrical work carried out since 1 January 2005 must comply with Part P of the Building Regulations. An installation with a rewireable board would receive a C2 (potentially dangerous) or C1 (immediately dangerous) code on an Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR).

Does Your Board Have a Wooden Backing?

Open the consumer unit cupboard and look behind or below the board. If you can see unfinished timber, MDF, or plywood as the mounting surface, the installation predates modern non-combustible requirements. This is not something to defer — book a health check and get it assessed.

What BS 7671:2018 (18th Edition) Actually Requires

BS 7671 is the UK's national standard for electrical installations — widely known as the "18th Edition" wiring regulations, updated in 2018 and applying to all new and upgraded domestic installations. When we fit a new consumer unit, it must meet these requirements:

  • Non-combustible enclosure. The consumer unit housing must be made from steel or another non-combustible material. Plastic enclosures have been banned in new domestic installations since January 2016. A plastic board is not illegal to leave in place, but it will fail an EICR.
  • RCD protection on most circuits. All socket outlet circuits, bathroom circuits, and circuits in high-risk zones must be RCD-protected. We achieve this with individual RCBOs (see below).
  • Surge Protection Device (SPD). Amendment 2 to BS 7671 (2022) made SPD provision a default requirement for new installations where loss of supply would cause serious consequences. We include an SPD as standard on all new boards.
  • Correct earthing and main protective bonding. Gas pipes, water pipes, and any structural steel must be bonded to earth with correctly sized conductors. We verify all bonding before the job is complete.
  • Labelled circuits. Every way in the new consumer unit must be clearly labelled. We use laser-printed labels — no handwriting that fades in a year.

What You Get with a New All-RCBO Board

There are three main types of compliant consumer unit: split-load (one RCD covers half the circuits), high-integrity (two or three RCDs), and all-RCBO (every circuit has its own combined MCB and RCD). For most homes we recommend the all-RCBO option — here is why, and what it delivers:

  • Individual circuit protection. Each RCBO (Residual Current Breaker with Overcurrent protection) combines an MCB and an RCD in one device. A fault on the kitchen ring main trips only the kitchen circuit — not the lights, the upstairs sockets, or anything else.
  • SPD (Surge Protection Device). A Type 2 SPD clamps voltage spikes caused by nearby lightning strikes or grid switching events that would otherwise destroy TVs, laptops, smart home hubs, and white goods.
  • Steel enclosure. Non-combustible by design — meets and exceeds the 18th Edition requirement.
  • Labelled circuits, left and right. Every breaker is identified by room and appliance. Invaluable for future maintenance.
  • Verified earth bonding. We check and, where necessary, upgrade the main bonding to gas and water services before the job is signed off.
  • Electrical Installation Certificate (EIC). Your legal record of compliance, registered with NAPIT and Building Control. Essential documentation when you sell your home.
  • NAPIT Part P notification — included at no extra charge. We handle the Building Control registration ourselves. You don't need to do anything.

Selling Your Home? A Rewireable Board Will Flag on Survey

Conveyancing surveyors acting for mortgage lenders across Wells, Bath, Frome, Midsomer Norton, and the wider Somerset area routinely flag outdated consumer units as a condition of sale. An older board — especially one with rewireable fuses, no RCD protection, or a plastic enclosure — will typically generate a recommendation for an EICR, and if the EICR returns C1 or C2 codes, the lender may refuse to release funds until remedial work is complete.

The result is a forced upgrade under time pressure, at a price that reflects your urgency, with someone else's preferred contractor. Planning ahead and upgrading before you market the property is almost always the better move. A compliant consumer unit with an EIC and NAPIT registration certificate is a positive selling point, not just an absence of a problem.

Home insurance is another factor. Insurers are increasingly asking about consumer unit age and compliance when you make or renew a claim. A non-compliant board can give grounds to dispute a payout. A £450–£850 upgrade protects a six-figure asset.

What the Job Involves: A Typical One-Day Upgrade

A standard domestic consumer unit upgrade — replacing a rewireable board with a new all-RCBO unit — takes 4 to 6 hours for most homes. Here is how the day runs:

  1. Pre-work survey (30–45 minutes). We walk the property, count circuits, confirm earthing arrangements, check bonding to gas and water, and identify any circuits that may need remedial work before they can be reconnected to the new board.
  2. Power off. We coordinate with you to isolate the supply. For most domestic jobs we work from a DNO isolation at the meter — no prior arrangement with Western Power Distribution is needed for a straightforward upgrade.
  3. Old board out. The existing consumer unit is removed. If it is a rewireable type, we photograph it for the certification record before it goes.
  4. New board fitted. The replacement metal consumer unit is fixed to the wall, circuits are landed, and all connections are torqued to specification. SPD installed. Labels applied.
  5. Testing — every circuit. We test insulation resistance, earth fault loop impedance, prospective fault current, RCD trip times, and polarity for every single circuit before power is restored. Nothing is skipped.
  6. Power on, demonstration. We restore supply, walk you through the new board, explain how to operate it if a trip occurs, and answer any questions.
  7. Certification and notification. You receive your Electrical Installation Certificate before we leave. We register the work with NAPIT and Building Control within 30 days — usually faster. You will receive a completion certificate directly from Building Control.
