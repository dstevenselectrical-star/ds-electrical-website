How Much Does a Full House Rewire Cost in 2026?

Real price anchors from a NAPIT approved Somerset electrician. No hidden day rates, no “from” figures that triple by week two — here is exactly what you should expect to pay and what is included.

By DS Electrical · 23 April 2026 · 8 min read

A full house rewire is one of the most significant investments you can make in your home. It is also one of the jobs where pricing is most opaque — quotes ranging from £3,000 to £15,000 for a similar property are not unusual, and it is rarely obvious why. This guide breaks down what a rewire actually costs in 2026, what is included, and what will push the price in either direction.

Quick price guide for 2026

2-bed house: £2,800–£3,800  •  3-bed house: £3,500–£5,500  •  4-bed house: £5,000–£8,500
Listed or period properties typically add 25–30% to these figures. All prices include consumer unit, full testing, BS 7671 certification, and NAPIT registration.

What a Full Rewire Actually Includes

When we quote for a rewire via our domestic electrical service, the price covers everything needed to take a property from old wiring to a fully compliant, certified installation. Here is what that means in practice:

  • New consumer unit (fuseboard) — a metal-encased unit with RCBO protection on every circuit and surge protection device (SPD), fully compliant with BS 7671:2018 Amendment 2
  • All first-fix cabling — every new cable run throughout the property, chased into walls where needed, or routed under floorboards and across ceiling voids
  • All second-fix work — fitting sockets, switches, and light fittings to every room; we fit whatever you supply or can supply a standard white plate finish
  • New earthing and bonding — main protective bonding to gas and water services, supplementary bonding in kitchens and bathrooms where required
  • Smoke and heat detection — mains-powered Grade D interlinked alarms to BS 5839-6 throughout
  • Full BS 7671 testing and certification — every circuit tested, an Electrical Installation Certificate (EIC) issued before we leave
  • NAPIT Part P notification — we register the work with Building Control on your behalf; no separate application needed
  • Light decoration repair — chases are filled flush and made good; final skim is your plasterer's job, but we leave a tidy surface

Listed and Period Properties

Grade II listed buildings and properties in conservation areas typically add 25–30% to the above figures. The reasons are practical: surface conduit may be required instead of chasing (to protect historic fabric), additional planning liaison can be needed, and the materials used must respect the character of the building. We have rewired period farmhouses, Victorian terraces, and Georgian town houses across the Wells, Bath, and Frome areas — and can advise on the best approach during the survey.

What Pushes the Price Up

Solid walls and stone construction

Plasterboard stud walls can be drilled through quickly. Solid brick or Mendip stone walls need chasing — cutting a channel, bedding the cable, filling, and making good. That is slow work, and it leaves more for your plasterer to skim afterwards.

Occupied property

Working around furniture, covering floors every morning, and maintaining live power to a freezer or ring main while rewiring others adds time to every day. Empty properties are typically 15–20% faster to rewire.

Additional circuits alongside

If you are also adding an EV charger first-fix, Cat6 data cabling for home networking, or a structured lighting system, this adds cost but saves you a second visit and a second bout of disruption. We always advise doing these alongside a rewire rather than returning later.

Complex earthing and bonding

Older properties sometimes have inadequate or missing main protective bonding to gas and water services. Where this needs upgrading, it adds to the job but is non-negotiable for a compliant installation.

How the Process Works

A full domestic rewire follows a clear sequence. Understanding this helps you plan around the work and set expectations for your plasterer and decorator.

  1. Free site survey — we walk through your property, count circuits, assess wall construction, check the existing consumer unit, and note any particular requirements. You receive a written fixed-price quote within 48 hours.
  2. First fix — all cables are run throughout the property. Walls are chased where needed, floorboards lifted to run under, and back boxes fitted for sockets and switches. At the end of first fix, the property looks rough — this is normal.
  3. Plastering — your plasterer skims the chased walls. This is a separate trade; we fill chases flush before they arrive.
  4. Second fix — once the plaster is dry, we return to fit sockets, switches, light fittings, and the new consumer unit, and connect everything up. The property starts to look finished again.
  5. Test and certificate — every circuit is tested to BS 7671:2018: insulation resistance, earth fault loop impedance, RCD trip times, polarity. Your EIC is issued the same day, and we register the work with NAPIT and Building Control.

For a standard 3-bed property, the total elapsed time from first fix to certificate is typically 5–10 working days of electrical work, spread over two visits (first fix, then second fix after plastering). Plastering is between our two visits, so the full project from start to decorated finish is usually 3–6 weeks depending on your plasterer's availability.

What Is NOT Included in a Rewire Quote

  • Final plaster skim — we fill and make good, but the finish coat is your plasterer's job. Budget separately for this.
  • Decorating — painting after plastering is a separate trade entirely.
  • Light fittings — we fit what you supply. Fitting costs vary; fittings themselves are priced separately or supplied by you.
  • Kitchen appliances — we run and connect the circuits for cooker, hob, dishwasher, washing machine. The appliances themselves are not included.
  • Flooring replacement — we lift carpets or floor coverings to access sub-floor space and relay them, but final reinstatement of hard flooring is excluded.

Does a rewire add value to your home?

Yes, consistently. An unsatisfactory EICR or visible old wiring (rubber-insulated, fabric-sheathed, round-pin sockets) will be flagged by surveyors and will either delay or reduce the price on a sale. A rewired property with a current EIC removes that block entirely. Our customers in Wells, Shepton Mallet, and Bath regularly tell us the rewire made their sale straightforward where the previous vendor had struggled.

Do You Need a Full Rewire, or Just a Partial One?

Not every property needs a full rewire. If you have a modern consumer unit but older upstairs wiring, or rubber-insulated cables only in one part of the house, a partial rewire covering just those areas can be a cost-effective alternative. Partial rewires typically run at 40–60% of a full rewire price, but they require a post-works EICR to confirm the remaining original installation is still satisfactory.

If you are unsure whether you need a full or partial rewire, a diagnostic visit is the right starting point. We will test the existing installation, assess the condition of the wiring, and give you an honest recommendation — including whether doing nothing for now is a viable option.
