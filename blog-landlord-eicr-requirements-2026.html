Landlord EICR Requirements 2026: What the Law Says and What Fails Most

The Electrical Safety Standards Regulations 2020 made five-yearly EICR inspections compulsory for every private rented property in England. Here is exactly what the law requires, what remedial deadlines you face, and the five defects we see most often on rental properties in Somerset.

By DS Electrical · 23 April 2026 · 7 min read

What Is an EICR?

An Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR) is a formal inspection and test of all the fixed electrical wiring in a property — the circuits, consumer unit, earthing system, and accessories. A qualified electrician tests everything against BS 7671:2018 (the IET Wiring Regulations) and assigns observations one of three codes:

CodeMeaningRequired action
C1Danger present — risk of injuryRemedy immediately, before leaving site
C2Potentially dangerousRemedy within 28 days
C3Improvement recommendedNo legal obligation, but worth addressing

A property receives an Unsatisfactory result if any C1 or C2 observations are recorded. Only a property with zero C1 or C2 codes is classed as Satisfactory.

The Law: What Landlords Must Do in 2026

The Electrical Safety Standards in the Private Rented Sector (England) Regulations 2020

Since 1 April 2021 these Regulations apply to all private tenancies in England. Scotland and Wales have their own equivalent requirements. The core obligations are:

1. Inspect every five years (or at change of tenancy)

You must have a valid EICR in place at all times. If your current report is approaching its five-year anniversary — or if it was issued under a previous tenancy — you need to commission a fresh inspection. The report must be carried out by a qualified person: someone competent to inspect and test to BS 7671 (typically holding the City & Guilds 2391 or equivalent qualification).

2. Provide a copy to tenants and prospective tenants

You must give a copy of the report to your existing tenant within 28 days of the inspection. Any new tenant must receive a copy before they move in. If a prospective tenant requests a copy in writing, you must supply it within 28 days.

3. Supply a copy to the local authority within 7 days of request

Your local council can request a copy of the EICR at any time. You have seven days to provide it. Failure to comply can result in a fine of up to £30,000.

4. Carry out remedial work within 28 days (or sooner for C1)

If the report records any C1 or C2 observations, you must arrange remedial works within 28 days of the inspection date, or sooner if the report specifies it. C1 dangers must be made safe immediately — in practice, the inspector will either remedy the fault before leaving or isolate the circuit concerned. Once the remedial work is complete, you must obtain written confirmation from the electrician and provide this to your tenant and to the council on request.

The Five Things That Fail Most Often on Rental Properties

We carry out landlord EICRs across Wells, Bath, Shepton Mallet, Frome, Radstock, and Midsomer Norton. Over and over, the same defects come up — particularly on properties built before the 1990s that have not been rewired. Here is what to expect.

1. No RCD Protection on Socket Circuits

Residual Current Devices detect faults to earth and cut power in milliseconds — fast enough to prevent a fatal shock. The current wiring regulations (BS 7671:2018) require RCD protection on all socket-outlet circuits. Many older properties have consumer units with only MCBs (miniature circuit breakers) and no RCDs at all. This is almost always recorded as a C2 — potentially dangerous — and requires either a consumer unit upgrade or the addition of individual RCBOs on each socket circuit.

2. Old Fuseboxes with Rewireable Fuses

Rewireable fuse boards — sometimes called Wylex boards or old wire fuse boards — provide no RCD protection whatsoever. They also use fuse wire rather than modern circuit breakers, which means faults can go undetected for longer. We commonly see these in pre-1970s terraces in Frome and Radstock. A fuseboard of this type will typically attract a C2 or C1 observation and will require a full consumer unit replacement to clear the report.

3. Missing Earth Bonding on Gas and Water Pipework

Main protective bonding connects the incoming metal gas pipe and water pipe to the main earth terminal of the consumer unit. Its purpose is to ensure that if a fault energises any pipework, the voltage difference across it cannot give an electric shock. Bonding is frequently absent on properties where the meter positions have been moved, where kitchens have been refitted, or where the original installation predates modern regulations. This is a C2 observation and a relatively low-cost remedial fix — usually under two hours of work.

4. Damaged Sockets and Accessories with Exposed Live Parts

Cracked faceplates, loose socket covers, and accessories where the internal terminals are accessible all count as C1 observations — immediate dangers. These are common in high-turnover rentals where damage accumulates between tenancies. A responsible periodic inspection before each new tenancy will catch these, but we find them regularly during formal EICRs on properties that have not been inspected for several years.

5. Outside Sockets Without Adequate IP Protection

Socket outlets in exposed outdoor positions must be to at least IP44 rating to prevent ingress of water. Ordinary indoor sockets fitted on external walls or in outbuildings without IP-rated enclosures are a C2 observation. We frequently find standard indoor sockets in garden sheds, outbuildings, and on exterior walls in properties across Castle Cary and Bruton. The fix is straightforward — replace with a weatherproof outdoor socket and ensure the circuit has RCD protection — but it is one of the easiest defects to overlook without a professional inspection.

What Happens After a Fail?

An Unsatisfactory result does not mean you need to evict your tenant or halt a new tenancy. It means you have 28 days (or less for C1) to arrange the remedial works. Once the work is done, the electrician who carried out the remedials issues a Minor Electrical Installation Works Certificate or, where substantial work was done, an Electrical Installation Certificate. You then provide this documentation to your tenant.

Do not ignore an Unsatisfactory report

If a local authority inspection reveals that you have an Unsatisfactory EICR and have not carried out remedial works within the required timeframe, you can be served with a remedial notice. Non-compliance with that notice can lead to fines of up to £30,000. Your landlord insurance may also be invalidated if you knowingly allow a tenant to occupy a property with known electrical hazards.
