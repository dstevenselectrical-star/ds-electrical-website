Section 01

Why this guide — UK rental laws since 2020

Three sets of rules now sit between a landlord and a tenanted property. They tightened sharply between 2020 and 2022, and most non-compliance happens because nothing visibly changed in the wiring — only the paperwork around it did.

  • England — Electrical Safety Standards in the Private Rented Sector (England) Regulations 2020. In force from 1 June 2020 for new tenancies and 1 April 2021 for all existing tenancies. Applies to every private landlord with tenants in occupation.
  • Wales — Renting Homes (Wales) Act 2016, fitness regulations effective 1 December 2022. Brought EICR and interlinked-alarm requirements broadly into line with England.
  • Scotland — Repairing Standard (Scottish Government 2017, updated 2022). Required EICRs every 5 years on tenanted properties from March 2017; interlinked alarms required in all Scottish homes from February 2022.

Penalties for non-compliance. Local authorities can issue civil penalties of up to £30,000 per breach, serve cessation orders banning a property from being let, and arrange remedial works themselves with costs recovered from the landlord. Buildings and contents insurance is routinely voided where a valid EICR cannot be produced after a fire or claim.

Who’s affected? Every private landlord with a tenant in occupation — furnished or unfurnished, single dwellings, HMOs, blocks, holiday lets where there is a tenancy agreement. Owner-occupiers are out of scope. Social housing has its own equivalent inspection regime.
Section 02

EICR — the core requirement

The Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR) is the document the local authority and your insurer will ask for first. Everything else in this guide flows from it.

  • Required at the start of every new tenancy and renewed at least every 5 years.
  • Must be carried out by a qualified, competent person — in practice, City & Guilds 2391 (Inspection & Testing) plus current registration with NAPIT or NICEIC.
  • Test schedule and codes follow BS 7671 18th Edition + Amendment 2:2022.

Defect codes — what each one means and how fast it must be fixed:

C1

Danger present

Risk of injury exists. Make safe at inspection or isolate. Remediate within 28 days.

C2

Potentially dangerous

Could become dangerous. Remediate within 28 days. Renders the report Unsatisfactory.

C3

Improvement recommended

No statutory deadline. Does not affect the Satisfactory outcome. Plan into next maintenance window.

FI

Further investigation

A potential defect that cannot be fully assessed without opening up. Investigate within 28 days.

Section 03

Smoke alarms — BS 5839-6 Grade D

Battery-only alarms have not been compliant for English private rentals since October 2015. The standard for new and replacement installs is BS 5839-6 Grade D — mains-powered with a tamper-resistant backup, interlinked across the dwelling.

  • One alarm on every storey used as living accommodation.
  • Heat alarm in the kitchen (smoke alarms nuisance-trip on cooking).
  • CO alarm in any room with a fixed combustion appliance (gas boiler, gas cooker, solid-fuel stove, oil burner).
  • All alarms interlinked — one trips, all sound.

Country-by-country rules: England has required mains-interlinked alarms in private rentals since October 2015 (Smoke and Carbon Monoxide Alarm Regulations). Scotland extended the same rule to all homes in February 2022. Wales requires it under the Renting Homes Act 2022 from December 2022.

Responsibility: the landlord installs and replaces; the tenant carries out the routine button-test. Test every alarm at the start of every new tenancy and log it — unwitnessed alarms are one of the easiest fail points in an inspection.

Section 04

PAT testing of portable appliances

PAT testing isn’t named in the Electrical Safety Standards Regulations — but the Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS) and the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 both require landlord-supplied appliances to be safe. Annual portable-appliance testing is the recognised, defensible way to evidence that. Most insurers expect it.

  • Cover every landlord-supplied portable appliance — fridges, freezers, kettles, microwaves, lamps, washing machines, hoovers.
  • Tested annually, kept-on-property records.
  • Tenant-owned appliances are not the landlord’s responsibility.

Indicative cost: from £4 – £8 per appliance + VAT @ 20%. A typical furnished 2-bed flat usually falls in the £40 – £80 range. We bring a calibrated PAT tester, label every appliance with a pass-date sticker, and issue a PDF schedule on the day. PAT testing service page →

Section 05

EV charging — HMOs and flats

The grant landscape changed in April 2022. The original homeowner EVHS scheme closed; what remains is targeted at renters, flat-owners, HMO landlords and commercial property.

  • OZEV EV chargepoint grant for renters & flat-owners — up to £350 + VAT per chargepoint. Open to landlords installing on dwellings let to tenants, and to flat-owners in blocks. Replaced the homeowner EVHS in April 2022.
  • Workplace Charging Scheme (WCS) — £350 per socket, up to 40 sockets per applicant. Open to commercial landlords, HMO operators, and businesses with off-street parking.
  • DNO notification mandatory before any new EV charger circuit (Engineering Recommendation G100 / G98).
  • Type A RCBO required under BS 7671 18th Edition Amendment 2 — we never specify Type B for residential EV.

Both schemes are administered by OZEV and renewed annually. The grant is paid to the installer and netted off the customer invoice — you don’t claim it back yourself. Full pricing on our EV chargers page and the 2026 EV charger cost guide.

Section 06

Records keeping

Compliance is what you can prove on the day a local authority asks. The Regulations specify exact deadlines for each piece of paperwork.

  • EICR certificate — kept for at least 28 days after a tenancy ends. We recommend 6 years for insurance and CCJ cover.
  • Tenant copy within 28 days of the inspection. New tenants must receive a copy before they occupy.
  • Local authority on request — you must supply within 7 days of a written request.
  • Smoke alarm test log — date-stamped record at every new tenancy.
  • PAT certificates — annual schedule of every tested appliance with serial numbers.
  • Building Regulations Compliance Certificate for any notifiable Part P work — lodged by NAPIT, posted within 30 days.

Every certificate we issue is delivered as a searchable PDF the day of the test, with a paper copy posted by NAPIT within 30 days for your hard file.

Section 07

New build, refurb & change-of-use

Anything that adds, replaces, or significantly alters fixed wiring is notifiable under Part P of the Building Regulations in dwellings.

  • Always notifiable: new circuits, fuse-board (consumer unit) replacements, work in special locations — kitchens, bathrooms, shower rooms, outdoor wiring.
  • Two notification routes: Building Control (manual, fee-paying, slow), or a Competent Person Scheme installer (NAPIT, NICEIC, ELECSA) who self-certifies. The Compliance Certificate is lodged by the scheme operator within 30 days.
  • Change-of-use — converting a single dwelling to flats, or commercial to residential — triggers a full design review under BS 7671 plus Building Control sign-off.

Section 08

Common landlord traps

The most-frequent compliance failures we see on first inspection of a portfolio property:

  • Letting an EICR expire beyond 5 years — insurance is voided the moment the renewal date passes, even if no claim has been made yet.
  • Letting agent claims they “handled it” with no PDF certificate copy — the legal duty stays with the landlord. Always demand the certificate, verify the inspector’s NAPIT or NICEIC registration number, and check 2391 is on the report.
  • Buying a property with a stale EICR — the previous landlord’s expiry now resets to your problem on completion day. Treat any pre-purchase EICR older than 18 months as “assume expired”.
  • Not testing the smoke alarms at the start of every new tenancy — this is one of the easiest enforcement wins for a council inspector.
  • Old plastic-bodied consumer unit still in service — metal-clad enclosures have been mandatory on new domestic boards under BS 7671 Amendment 3 since January 2016. A plastic board is almost always a C2 on the EICR.
  • RCD-only board with no RCBOs — one fault trips the whole house. A modern dual-RCD or full-RCBO board with SPD is now the BS 7671 Amendment 2 default.
  • EV charger fitted before tenancy with a Type AC or Type B RCBO — only Type A is compliant for residential single-phase charging.
