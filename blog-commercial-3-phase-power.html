Commercial 3-Phase Power Explained:
When Your Business Needs an Upgrade

Most businesses start on a single-phase supply. But as loads grow — bigger machinery, multiple EV chargers, server rooms — single-phase quietly becomes the bottleneck. Here is how to know when you have outgrown it.

By DS Electrical · 23 April 2026 · 7 min read

The difference between single-phase and three-phase power is one of the most misunderstood topics in commercial electrical work. Business owners are often told they need a three-phase supply without being given a clear explanation of why — or whether it is actually true in their case. This guide gives you the plain English version, the warning signs that you are running short, and what the upgrade process looks like in practice.

Single-Phase vs Three-Phase: The Plain English Version

The electricity grid in the UK distributes power using three separate alternating current (AC) waves — the three phases — that are offset from one another by 120 degrees. This is how the National Grid transmits power efficiently over long distances.

A single-phase supply — what almost every domestic property and many smaller commercial premises receive — taps into just one of those three waves. Your maximum usable power is determined by that single line: typically 100A at 230V, giving you around 23kW of usable capacity before you start approaching the limits of the supply.

A three-phase supply uses all three waves simultaneously. Instead of one 230V line, you have three — plus a neutral. The voltage between any two phases is 400V, and because the load is shared evenly across three conductors, you can draw three times the current without overloading any single wire. Practical usable capacity jumps to 60–70kW or more, depending on your agreed supply capacity with the Distribution Network Operator (DNO).

The key point: three-phase does not mean you need three times the cable size per circuit. Each phase carries the same current as a single-phase supply — the gain comes from spreading the load, not from brute-forcing more current through one wire.

Signs You Are Maxing Out Your Single-Phase Supply

Single-phase capacity problems tend to reveal themselves gradually. These are the four most common warning signs we see on commercial jobs across Somerset:

1. Breakers Tripping Under Full Load

If your main breaker or RCD trips when machinery starts up or when you run several large loads at once — ovens, compressors, welding equipment — your supply is being pushed beyond its rated current. A single trip could be a nuisance; repeated trips under normal operating conditions are a structural problem, not a fault to reset and ignore.

2. Lights Dimming When Machinery Starts

A noticeable dip in lighting the moment a large motor kicks in — a compressor, a refrigeration unit, an extraction fan — is a voltage sag. The motor draws a high inrush current on start-up, momentarily pulling down the supply voltage for everything else on the circuit. On a properly provisioned supply this should not be perceptible. If you can see it, your supply is undersized for the load.

3. Voltage Dips Affecting Sensitive Equipment

Voltage dips that are invisible to the naked eye can still cause havoc with IT equipment, CNC machinery, or any device with a sensitive control board. If you are seeing unexplained resets, data corruption, or machinery faults that do not correspond to any obvious cause, have an electrician carry out power quality monitoring before assuming the equipment is faulty.

4. You Cannot Install the Equipment You Need

Some equipment simply requires three-phase power to operate at all — not because the designers were being awkward, but because the motors are wound for three-phase and cannot be run from a single-phase supply without an expensive phase converter. If a supplier's specification sheet says "400V three-phase," you need three-phase before you can install it.

When Do You Actually Need Three-Phase?

Not every business that is struggling with capacity needs a three-phase supply — sometimes load management, a supply upgrade within the existing single-phase infrastructure, or spreading loads across circuits is the right answer. But there are situations where three-phase is the only sensible solution:

  • Continuous load over 20kW. If your business regularly draws 20kW or more — manufacturing, catering, data centres, large workshop equipment — three-phase is almost always more cost-effective than trying to manage within single-phase limits.
  • Three-phase machinery. CNC machining centres, large lathes, industrial compressors, refrigeration plant, and most motors above 4kW are wound for three-phase. Running them from single-phase via a converter wastes energy and introduces reliability risk.
  • Multiple large EV chargers. Each rapid or ultra-rapid commercial EV charger can draw 22kW–50kW. Two or three chargers running simultaneously will overwhelm a standard single-phase supply. If you are planning a commercial charging facility, three-phase is the only practical foundation.
  • Server rooms and data infrastructure. Server UPS systems, precision cooling, and high-density rack power all benefit from three-phase distribution — it allows balanced loading and reduces the risk of neutral overload that can occur with large single-phase IT loads.
  • Future-proofing a new premises. If you are fitting out a commercial unit or agricultural building and anticipate growth, specifying three-phase at first fix costs far less than retrofitting later. The DNO application fee and the groundworks are the biggest variable costs; the incremental cost of three-phase cabling over single-phase at first fix is modest.

The DNO Application Process

Getting a three-phase supply to a commercial premises involves your Distribution Network Operator (DNO) — the company responsible for the cables in the street outside your building. In Somerset, that is Western Power Distribution (WPD), now trading as National Grid Electricity Distribution. In some border areas you may fall under Scottish and Southern Electricity Networks (SSEN).

The process broadly works like this:

  1. Load assessment. We work with you to calculate your maximum demand — existing loads plus any planned additions. This figure goes to the DNO as the basis for their capacity offer.
  2. DNO application. We submit a new connection application on your behalf. For a standard commercial upgrade from single-phase to three-phase, the DNO will carry out a network study to confirm there is sufficient three-phase capacity in your part of the network.
  3. Quotation and acceptance. The DNO issues a quotation for the connection work — running a new service cable, upgrading the meter point, and updating the cut-out at your premises. You have a period to accept.
  4. On-site work by DS Electrical. Once the DNO has upgraded the external supply, we install the new distribution board, metering arrangement, and internal wiring to take advantage of the three-phase supply.
  5. Certification and sign-off. We issue an Electrical Installation Certificate (EIC) covering all the new internal work, notified to Building Control via NAPIT registration.

How Long Does It Take?

From initial application to a live three-phase supply, allow 4–12 weeks for a standard commercial upgrade. The DNO is the variable — network studies, quotation turnaround, and street or excavation work can all add time. Starting the process early, before you are desperate, is the single most important thing you can do. We handle the DNO application and liaison so you are not chasing it yourself.

In these situations it is worth asking whether there is an alternative connection point that would reduce the DNO element.
